The first neighborhood results show that open sets containing a point are neighborhoods, open neighborhoods admit a pointwise characterization, finite intersections of neighborhoods remain neighborhoods, and openness of a set is equivalent to every one of its points possessing that set as a neighborhood. Concretely, the file proves the pattern

\[ x\in U,\ U\text{ open } \implies U\in \mathcal N(x), \]
\[ V_1,V_2\in \mathcal N(x) \implies V_1\cap V_2\in \mathcal N(x), \]

and

\[ U\text{ open } \iff \forall x\in U,\ U\in \mathcal N(x). \]
Definition 3.3 Neighborhood bases
✓
#

Lean declarations

  • LeanTopology.NeighborhoodBasis.IsNeighborhoodBasis_2_5

A family \(\mathcal{U}\) is a neighborhood basis at \(x\) if every element of \(\mathcal{U}\) is a neighborhood of \(x\) and every neighborhood of \(x\) contains at least one member of \(\mathcal{U}\). Thus the defining conditions are

\[ \forall U\in \mathcal{U},\quad U\in \mathcal N(x), \qquad \forall V\in \mathcal N(x)\ \exists U\in \mathcal{U},\quad U\subseteq V. \]

Several standard neighborhood bases are formalized: all neighborhoods, all open neighborhoods, metric open balls, reciprocal-radius metric balls, open intervals in \(\mathbb {R}\), and singleton bases at isolated points in discrete spaces. Among the concrete families are

\[ \mathcal N(x), \qquad \mathcal N_{\mathrm{open}}(x), \qquad \{ B(x,r)\mid r{\gt}0\} , \qquad \{ B(x,1/n)\mid n\in \mathbb {N}_{{\gt}0}\} , \]

as well as the real-interval family

\[ \{ (x-\varepsilon ,x+\varepsilon )\mid \varepsilon {\gt}0\} \]

and singleton bases at isolated points.

Definition 3.7 First countability
✓
#

  • LeanTopology.NeighborhoodBasis.FirstCountable_2_12

A space is first countable if each point admits a countable neighborhood basis. Equivalently,

\[ \forall x\in X\ \exists (U_n)_{n\in \mathbb {N}} \quad \text{such that} \quad \forall V\in \mathcal N(x)\ \exists n,\ U_n\subseteq V. \]

Definition 3.9 Sequential convergence
✓
#

Lean declarations

  • LeanTopology.NeighborhoodBasis.TendstoSeq_2_16

A sequence \((x_n)\) converges to \(x\) if for every neighborhood \(V\) of \(x\) there exists \(N\) such that \(x_n\in V\) whenever \(n\ge N\). That is,

\[ x_n \to x \iff \forall V\in \mathcal N(x)\ \exists N\in \mathbb {N}\ \forall n\ge N, \quad x_n\in V. \]

Proposition 3.11 Testing convergence on a basis
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 3.3
  • Definition 3.9

Lean declarations

  • LeanTopology.NeighborhoodBasis.tendstoSeq_iff_neighborhoodBasis_2_18

A sequence converges to \(x\) if and only if it is eventually contained in every member of a chosen neighborhood basis at \(x\).

Proof ▶

If a sequence converges in the ordinary neighborhood sense, then every basis element is in particular a neighborhood, so the eventual-containment property follows immediately.

Conversely, suppose the eventual-containment property holds for a basis \(\mathcal{U}\) at \(x\). Let \(V\) be any neighborhood of \(x\). Since \(\mathcal{U}\) is a basis, there exists some \(U\in \mathcal{U}\) with \(U\subseteq V\). By hypothesis, the sequence is eventually in \(U\), hence eventually in \(V\). Therefore the original definition of convergence holds.
